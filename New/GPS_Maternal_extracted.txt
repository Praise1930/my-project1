reduce mortality rates in Mukono district, Uganda. It begins by establishing the background of the study, contextualizing the maternal health crisis and the critical role of spatial technology in modern healthcare. It then transitions to the problem statement, which critically analyses the causes and effects of spatial invisibility within current emergency maternal care workflows. Following the problem statement, the study’s primary and specific objectives are defined to outline the technical milestones of the research. Furthermore, the scope is established to provide clear subject, geographical, and time boundaries for the project and finally the significance of the study is then presented through a detailed justification of its practical and theoretical contributions to the field of health informatics. 1.2 Background to the StudyThe field of maternal health informatics has transitioned from static record-keeping to a dynamic integration of the Internet of Things (IoT), Global Positioning Systems (GPS), and real-time analytics. Globally, the World Health Organization (WHO) reports approximately 800 daily maternal deaths from preventable causes, with 95% occurring in low-resource settings (World Health Organization, 2023). In emerging economies like India, the deployment of GPS-tracked ambulances and IoT-enabled wearable sensors has proven effective in reducing the Second Delay, the critical interval required to reach a health facility (Thaddeus & Maine, 1994). In Africa, digital health initiatives have primarily centred on mHealth. While programs like Kenya’s Linda Mama have increased service uptake; they remain fundamentally location-blind.In Uganda, where the maternal mortality ratio stands at 284 deaths per 100,000 live births (Uganda Bureau of Statistics, 2022), existing interventions such as UNICEF’s SMS-based reminders and Kampala’s eHealth referral pilots lack real-time geospatial integration. This technical deficit prevents healthcare providers from tracking patients in labour or identifying precise coordinates during obstetric emergencies.The current influence of GPS and IoT on Ugandan maternal health is restricted to high-level urban pilots, leaving a significant technological gap in peri-urban and rural districts. Existing systems function as static databases rather than active emergency dispatch tools, resulting in fatal navigation errors during complications like Postpartum Haemorrhage (PPH).Mukono District, the case study for this research, epitomizes these challenges. As a region characterized by poorly mapped rural villages in Nama and isolated island communities in Koome, verbal directions are frequently unreliable. Consequently, maternal referrals to Mukono General Hospital suffer from spatial invisibility. This study will aim to develop a GPS-enabled solution within Mukono to provide a scalable, data-driven model for reducing maternal mortality through precise, real-time location tracking.1.3 Statement of the ProblemDespite national interventions, Uganda’s maternal mortality ratio remains critically high at 284 deaths per 100,000 live births (Uganda Bureau of Statistics, 2022). In Mukono District, a primary driver of these fatalities is the Second Delay, the time lost in locating and transporting a mother during an obstetric emergency. Current response workflows are location-blind, relying on manual records and vague verbal directions, which prevents the timely dispatch of ambulances and Village Health Teams (VHTs). This lack of spatial visibility frequently turns manageable complications, such as Postpartum Haemorrhage, into avoidable tragedies. The root cause of this crisis is spatial information invisibility. The absence of a digital infrastructure to transmit precise GPS coordinates leads to fatal navigation errors in poorly mapped areas like Nama and isolated island communities like Koome. This is exacerbated by fragmented communication and the reliance on physical paper cards, which restricts access to vital clinical history during transit. Consequently, mothers lose the critical therapeutic window for intervention, often falling into inefficient referral loops between facilities that lack necessary resources. This systemic failure results in unassisted births and high risks of sepsis, primarily affecting rural mothers in Mukono and hindering the achievement of Sustainable Development Goal 3 (United Nations, 2015).To address these challenges, this research will propose a GPS-Enabled Maternal Health Information System for Mukono District. By integrating a mobile interface for mothers with a web-based hospital dashboard, the system will utilize GIS technology to provide real-time tracking and automated resource identification. This solution ensures a mother’s precise location is visible to medical responders before she arrives at the facility, effectively bridging the spatial gap in emergency maternal care. Despite the proliferation of mobile health (mHealth) initiatives in Uganda, such as UNICEF’s mTrac and various SMS-based antenatal reminder systems, a significant technological void remains in the area of real-time emergency navigation. Existing systems are fundamentally "location-blind," functioning primarily as static databases for record-keeping rather than active tools for emergency dispatch.Current literature focuses heavily on the clinical causes of maternal mortality, yet there is a lack of integrated research into how Geospatial Informatics can specifically resolve the Second Delay, the logistical time lost while health workers and ambulance drivers struggle to locate a mother in distress due to vague verbal directions or poorly mapped terrains. In Mukono District, the geographical isolation of island communities and rural sub-counties like Nama exacerbates this "spatial invisibility". This study will, therefore, address this gap by designing a system that synchronizes a mother’s real-time GPS coordinates with a centralized hospital dashboard, providing a first-of-its-kind template for event-driven spatial health informatics in Uganda’s public health sector.1.4 Objectives of the Study1.4.1 General ObjectiveThe main objective of this research is to design and develop a GPS-Enabled Maternal Health Information System for Mukono District to eliminate spatial invisibility and reduce the Second Delay during obstetric emergencies. 1.4.2 Specific Objectives(i) To study and analyse the strengths and weaknesses of the current maternal emergency response and referral workflows within Mukono General Hospital and its surrounding health centres.(ii) To determine and specify the functional and technical requirements for a real-time geospatial tracking and alert system tailored for the terrain of Mukono District.(iii) To design a GPS-integrated maternal health system consisting of a mobile emergency interface for mothers and a centralized navigation dashboard for hospital administrators.(iv) To implement, test, and validate the new system through simulated emergency scenarios to measure its effectiveness in improving response times and coordination.1.5 Research QuestionsTo achieve the objectives of this research, the following questions will be addressed; While medical protocols for treating complications exist, the inability to locate and reach a mother in distress remains a critical bottleneck. This research is essential because it moves maternal health from a reactive paper-based model to a proactive spatial-awareness model. Current maternal health interventions in Uganda primarily rely on mHealth (Mobile Health) platforms that focus on one-way communication, such as SMS reminders for antenatal visits. However, a significant technical gap exists, these systems are blind to the mother’s physical location during a crisis. Most existing studies focus on clinical causes of death but overlook the Geospatial Informatics required to solve the Second Delay which is reaching the facility (Faujdar & Kumar, 2020). There is currently no integrated platform in Uganda’s public hospitals that links a mother’s real-time GPS coordinates to a centralized hospital dashboard for immediate emergency dispatch. The implementation of this system offers immediate, tangible improvements to the maternal healthcare process: Precision Emergency Response: By replacing vague verbal directions with precise GPS coordinates, the system minimizes the time ambulances or Village Health Teams (VHTs) spend searching for a patient’s home. Intelligent Referral Mapping: The system can identify the nearest facility with available beds and the correct blood type, preventing the common referral merry-go-round where mothers are bounced between full hospitals. Data-Driven Policy: Aggregated GPS data will allow the Ministry of Health to identify mortality hotspots, enabling them to place new clinics or ambulances exactly where they are needed most. This study contributes to the academic body of knowledge in the following ways: GIS-Healthcare Integration: It provides a new framework for applying Geographic Information Systems to maternal health in resource-constrained environments. Low-Bandwidth Optimization: Academically, this research explores the synchronization of spatial data over GSM/USSD networks, providing a model for how complex GPS data can be transmitted in areas with poor internet connectivity. Informatics Evolution: It advances the study of Health Information Systems (HIS) by shifting the focus from static data storage to real-time, event-driven architecture. This research serves as a pioneer for Spatial Health Informatics in East Africa. By successfully integrating GPS tracking into public hospital workflows, this project provides a scalable template that can be adapted for other emergencies, such as accident response or epidemic tracking. It directly supports the Uganda National Development Plan (NDP III) (National Planning Authority, 2020), proving that homegrown, location-aware technology is the most effective tool for reducing the national maternal mortality ratio. 1.8 Chapter SummaryChapter One has established the foundational framework for this research proposal by identifying the urgent need for a spatial intervention in Uganda’s maternal healthcare. Early systems primarily focused on patient registration and aggregate reporting, limiting their utility in emergency scenarios. In Uganda, the Health Management Information System (HMIS) reflects this limitation, as it largely supports retrospective data analysis rather than real-time intervention. The emergence of mobile health (mHealth) technologies has significantly improved communication in maternal healthcare. This transforms distance from a physical barrier into an information failure. Empirical evidence from Uganda shows that delays in reaching facilities are a major contributor to maternal mortality, particularly in rural and peri-urban settings. In Mukono, poorly mapped settlements such as Nama and isolated island communities like Koome intensify this challenge. Thus, addressing the second delay requires not only transport solutions but also geospatial intelligence systems. 2.3.3 Addressing the Second Delay through Geospatial InformaticsThe focus of this research is specifically centred on mitigating the Second Delay. In the context of Mukono, this delay is not merely a product of physical distance, but of Spatial Invisibility, a technical gap where healthcare providers cannot digitally visualize or navigate to a patient's precise location.By applying Geospatial Integration, the framework transforms distance from a physical barrier into a manageable data point.
